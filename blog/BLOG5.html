Policy Shocks: U.S. Immigration, Remittance Taxes, and Their Ripple Effects on Latin America

The flow of remittances from the United States to Latin America is not just an economic transaction—it is a lifeline for millions of families and a critical pillar for national economies. However, recent and proposed shifts in U.S. immigration and remittance policies threaten to disrupt these flows, with far-reaching consequences across the region.

Changing Immigration Policies and Growing Uncertainty

The current U.S. administration has ushered in a wave of new immigration policies, many of which directly impact Latin American migrant workers. Legislation such as the Laken Riley Act, the WIRED Act, and the shortening of Temporary Protected Status (TPS) for Venezuelan immigrants have created an environment of uncertainty and fear. These changes threaten job security and legal status for hundreds of thousands of migrants, many of whom are primary providers for families back home.

This climate of insecurity has real economic consequences. Faced with the threat of deportation or job loss, many migrant workers are choosing to save money rather than send it home. In some cases, migrants may be forced to return to their countries of origin, eliminating their ability to send remittances altogether. The impact is most acute in countries where remittances make up a significant share of GDP, such as Honduras, El Salvador, Nicaragua, and Haiti.

Remittance Taxes: A New Threat to Household Incomes

On top of immigration enforcement, new proposals to tax remittances could further reduce the funds reaching Latin America. President Trump’s proposed 10% tax on remittances, along with state-level taxes in places like Oklahoma (and proposals in Florida, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and Arizona), would directly cut into the disposable income of families who rely on these transfers for basic needs. Such taxes, in addition to the already high fees (averaging around 6%), would dampen consumption-driven growth in countries like Mexico, El Salvador, and Haiti, and could push more remittance flows into informal or unregulated channels.

Economic and Social Ripple Effects

The consequences of these policy shocks are far-reaching:

  • Reduced Family Income: Any reduction in remittance flows directly threatens the well-being of recipient families.
  • Regional Instability: A drop in remittances could increase poverty and drive more people to attempt migration north, creating a cycle of instability.
  • Macroeconomic Impact: Any U.S. policy that reduces these flows risks undermining national economies and exacerbating social challenges—For example, remittances from the U.S accounted for 26.18% of Nicaragua’s GDP in 2023 and 25.58% of Honduras’ GDP in 2023.

Political and Policy Feedback Loops

Restrictive policies can create feedback loops: as remittances decline, economic hardship in recipient countries may increase, fueling further migration and instability. For example, anti-immigration measures in the U.S. have historically led to increased out-migration from politically unstable countries, as families seek new sources of income abroad.

The Stakes for Latin America

Remittances are a cornerstone of economic stability and social well-being in Latin America. U.S. policy shifts—whether through immigration enforcement or remittance taxation—have the power to disrupt lives and economies far beyond American borders. As these debates continue, the region’s future will be shaped not only by economic forces but by the political choices made in Washington and state capitals across the U.S.
