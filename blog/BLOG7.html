The Road Ahead: Digitalization, Partnerships, and Inclusive Growth in Latin American Remittances

As Latin America stands at the crossroads of financial innovation and persistent structural barriers, the future of remittances in the region will be defined by the pace of digitalization, the strength of partnerships, and the commitment to inclusive growth. The stakes are high: in 2024, Latin America and the Caribbean received approximately $160.9 billion in remittances, a lifeline that sustains millions of families and drives local economies.

Digitalization: Promise and Persistent Gaps

Digital remittances are steadily gaining ground, with global digital remittance senders surpassing cash for the first time (52% of total volume in 2023). However, Latin America lags behind, with only 43% of remittances sent digitally in 2023 and expectations to reach just over half in 2025. Despite the shift to online transactions, about 57% of remittances in the region are still paid out in cash, highlighting the enduring importance of physical currency—especially in rural and underserved communities.

The transition to digital is hampered by two major obstacles:

  • Infrastructure Gaps: Limited access to reliable internet and digital devices, particularly outside urban centers.
  • Trust and Financial Education: Many recipients are unfamiliar with digital tools or remain cautious due to a long history of financial instability and exclusion.

Partnerships: Building a Diverse Ecosystem

No single actor can solve the region’s remittance challenges alone. The remittance value chain is increasingly shaped by a diverse partnership ecosystem:

  • Banks struggle with legacy systems and regulatory constraints, making them slower to innovate.
  • Traditional Money Transfer Operators (MTOs) rely on costly agent networks, which are less competitive as consumers shift to digital-first alternatives.
  • Telcos and Retailers risk losing market share without digital expertise but can reach remote populations.
  • Digital-Only MTOs offer speed and lower costs but face low brand awareness and trust among traditional users.
  • Crypto Players introduce new rails but must overcome regulatory and trust hurdles.

Collaborations—such as Leap Financials’ partnership with MasterCard, embedding remittance requests into everyday platforms like WhatsApp—demonstrate how technology, convenience, and familiarity can converge to build trust and expand access. However, these innovations also raise critical concerns about data privacy and surveillance. If platforms like Meta are compelled to share remittance and geolocation data with government agencies such as ICE, trust in these services would be severely eroded. So, it’s a tricky situation.

Financial and Digital Inclusion: The Next Frontier

A critical challenge is bridging the financial and digital exclusion gap. Many Latin Americans remain unbanked or underbanked, relying on cash and informal networks. To address this, industry leaders stress the importance of:

  • Optionality: Offering both cash and digital solutions, ensuring users can choose what works best for their circumstances.
  • Education: Helping users understand and trust digital financial products, often through repeated use and clear, transparent communication.
  • Incentives and Loyalty: Providing rewards, discounts, or added value to encourage digital adoption and repeat engagement.

Innovation: Crypto, Stablecoins, and New Payment Rails

The region is also experimenting with new technologies, from stablecoins and central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) to blockchain-based payment rails. While crypto remittances accounted for less than 10% of flows in 2024, their rapid growth—900% from 2023 to 2024—signals potential for the future, especially if volatility and regulatory concerns can be addressed.

Policy and Regulation: A Moving Target

The regulatory environment will play a decisive role in shaping the future. Policies that foster competition, protect consumers, and encourage innovation—while addressing risks like fraud and money laundering—will be essential for sustainable growth. Industry voices emphasize the need for a level playing field between fintechs and traditional banks, as well as clear guidelines for emerging technologies.

Inclusive, Resilient Remittance Systems

The road ahead for remittances in Latin America is complex but full of potential. Digitalization, strategic partnerships, and a relentless focus on inclusion can transform the sector—reducing costs, increasing convenience, and empowering millions. The challenge will be to ensure that innovation reaches everyone, not just the digitally savvy, and that trust is built through transparency, education, and genuine value. With $85 billion in cash-based remittances still waiting to be digitized, the opportunity for impact is enormous—and the time to act is now.
