Cryptocurrency and Stablecoins: Hype, Reality, and Future Potential for Remittances in Latin America

The digital transformation of remittances in Latin America is increasingly intertwined with the rise of cryptocurrency and stablecoins. As the region seeks faster, cheaper, and more inclusive ways to move money across borders, crypto-based solutions are gaining attention. However, not without significant hurdles and skepticism.

The Promise of Crypto Remittances

Stablecoins, which are digital assets pegged to fiat currencies, have emerged as a promising alternative for remittance transfers. Unlike traditional cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin, stablecoins offer reduced volatility, making them more attractive for users who need predictability when sending money to family members abroad. The appeal of crypto remittances lies in their potential to lower transaction costs, speed up settlement times, and bypass some of the inefficiencies and fees imposed by traditional intermediaries.

Recent innovations highlight this potential. For example, Latin American remittance startup Féliz Pago uses the Circle USDC stablecoin to facilitate cross-border payments via WhatsApp, saving on foreign exchange costs and making transactions cheaper and faster than legacy systems like SWIFT. Similarly, Leap Financial has embedded crypto functionality into its AI assistant LOLA on WhatsApp, allowing users to send remittances as crypto through a familiar platform, which can help overcome trust barriers.

Adoption: Rapid Growth from a Small Base

Despite the promise, crypto remittances still account for less than 10% of global remittance flows. However, the sector is expanding rapidly, with a 900% growth rate in crypto remittances worldwide over the course of one year, from 2023 to 2024. Countries like Venezuela, Argentina, Brazil, Mexico, and El Salvador are seeing the highest levels of growth in crypto transfers. In El Salvador, where Bitcoin is legal tender, digital wallet remittances still represent only a small share of the total at just 1.62% as of March 2022, but the groundwork for broader adoption is being laid.

Barriers: Volatility, Complexity, and Trust

Several obstacles limit the widespread adoption of crypto for remittances in Latin America:

  • Volatility: While stablecoins mitigate some risk, traditional cryptocurrencies remain highly volatile, which can deter users seeking reliability.
  • Complexity: Many Latin Americans are unfamiliar with crypto markets, currencies, and exchange rates. In Argentina, El Salvador (pre-2021), and Brazil, less than 20% of the population has ever used any form of cryptocurrency.
  • Trust and Regulation: Trust in new financial technologies is low, especially given the region’s history of financial instability. Regulatory uncertainty, risks of scams, hacking, and money laundering also pose significant challenges. For crypto to become mainstream, governments and private sector players must implement robust regulations that protect users without stifling innovation.

Costs: Not Always Cheaper

While sending Bitcoin or Ethereum can be inexpensive in terms of network fees (often $0.75–$1.50 per transaction), there are still costs associated with wallets, cashing in and out, and exchange rate volatility. These factors mean that crypto remittances are not always cheaper for consumers, especially when compared to the most efficient traditional digital channels.

The Road Ahead: Stablecoins, CBDCs, and Familiar Platforms

Looking forward, the future of crypto remittances in Latin America may hinge on the adoption of stablecoins and central bank digital currencies (CBDCs), which offer the benefits of digital assets without the volatility of traditional crypto. Integrating these solutions into platforms that users already trust, such as WhatsApp, could accelerate adoption by lowering barriers related to education and trust.

For now, crypto remains a niche but rapidly growing segment of the remittance market. Its ultimate impact will depend on regulatory clarity, continued innovation, and the ability to build trust with users who have long relied on cash and traditional financial services.
