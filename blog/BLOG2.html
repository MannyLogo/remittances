The Importance of Remittances to the Latin American Economy

Remittances (a transfer of money from one country to another) are a critical economic and social lifeline for millions of families across Latin America. In 2024 alone, Latin America and the Caribbean received approximately $160.9 billion in remittances; a figure that not only highlights the scale of these flows but also their profound impact on the region’s development.

Remittances: A Pillar of National Economies

For countries like Honduras and El Salvador, remittances account for nearly one-fifth of national GDP, making them essential for economic stability. Even during the COVID-19 pandemic, when many expected a decline in remittance flows, the region saw a remarkable 27% growth in 2021. This resilience demonstrates how deeply remittances are woven into the fabric of Latin American economies.

How Remittances Are Used

88.1% of remittance funds are spent on daily necessities such as food, transportation, and basic services. Medical expenses account for 57.1% of remittance usage, while education expenses represent 31.4%. Other significant uses include debt repayment (20.1%), savings or business investment (13.8%), and home purchase or remodeling (13.1%).

“These transfers allow recipient families to improve their quality of life, access education and healthcare services, and in some cases, invest in their own businesses, generating a multiplier effect throughout local economies.” - (IntelliNews)

Remittances and Migration Policy Shocks

U.S. policy shifts have great impacts on remittances, as it is the main supplier of these funds. About 80% of Latin American remittances originate from the U.S., and changes in immigration or labor policy can have immediate ripple effects. For example, proposed remittance taxes or stricter immigration enforcement threaten to reduce disposable income for families in Mexico, El Salvador, Haiti, and other nations, dampening consumption-driven growth and potentially exacerbating poverty.

“Nations such as Nicaragua, Honduras, El Salvador, Guatemala, and Haiti derive between one-fifth and one-quarter of their GDP from these remittances.”  (Folha)

Remittance Flows: A Cushion Against Instability

Remittances are especially vital for the poorest households and the most politically unstable countries. In Mexico, for instance, the bottom 30% of households receive over 65% of total remittance flows, and most of this money is spent on essentials like food and housing. Any disruption to these flows—whether from economic downturns, policy changes, or increased migration enforcement—would have immediate and severe consequences for millions.

Sustaining Families, Fueling Growth

The economic and social impact of remittances in Latin America cannot be overstated. They are not just transfers of money; they are investments in health, education, and opportunity. As the region continues to grapple with economic challenges, migration pressures, and the ongoing digital transformation of financial services, the role of remittances as a stabilizing force remains as important as ever.
